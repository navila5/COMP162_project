Machine Learning

Model Choice for quantitative variable :

  • Ridge Regression

Independent Variable:

  • Science and Engineering: This is the number of Science and Engineering degrees awarded, regardless of gender. Including this helps the model understand how overall participation in S&E fields might relate to the size of the gender gap.
  • Non-S&E: This variable accounts for degrees in fields outside Science and Engineering. It may capture differences in interest or opportunities between genders in other academic areas and can help the model identify trends where a high number of Non S&E degrees correlates with gender imbalances in Science and Engineering.
  • State: The state in which the data is measured, which is treated as a categorical variable.

Dependent Variable:

  • Gap_S&E: This represents the gender gap in Science and Engineering bachelors degrees by U.S. state. It is calculated by subtracting the number of female degrees from the number of male degrees in each state. A higher value indicates a larger gap favoring males, while a lower or negative value indicates a smaller or reversed gap. This variable is what the model aims to predict.

Interpretation:

  • The model has a high R score of 0.967 and a relatively low RMSE of 42,029, which suggests that it does a good job at predicting the gender gap in Science and Engineering degrees. This indicates that the model captures most of the variability in the target variable and is effective for this task. Since Ridge regression includes regularization, it helps prevent overfitting by penalizing overly complex models. The high R and low RMSE together suggest that the model is not underfitting either. Given these strong performance metrics, I'd recommend this model to be used for future predictions.

y = y.loc[X['State']]

X = pd.get_dummies(X, drop_first=True)

X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = model_selection.train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.2, random_state=123)

ridge_model = linear_model.Ridge(alpha=1.0)

ridge_model.fit(X_train, y_train)

y_pred = ridge_model.predict(X_test)

mse = metrics.mean_squared_error(y_test, y_pred)
rmse = math.sqrt(mse)
r2 = metrics.r2_score(y_test, y_pred)

Model Choice for Categorical variable:

  • Logistic Regression

Independent Variable:

  • Science and Engineering: This represents the number of Science and Engineering degrees earned. Including this variable allows the model to assess how involvement in S&E fields relates to gender, potentially capturing trends where one gender is more represented in these disciplines.

  • Non-S&E: This variable tracks the number of degrees earned in non Science and Engineering fields. It provides insight into gender representation in alternative academic areas and helps the model identify whether a preference for or against these fields correlates with gender.

Dependent Variable:

  • Sex: This is the gender of the individual, where 0 stands for male and 1 stands for female. The models goal is to predict someone's gender based on their degree focus. By doing this, it tries to find patterns in how men and women differ in their academic choices.

Interpretation:

  • The logistic regression model performs well, with about 91% accuracy on the training data and nearly 87% accuracy on the test data. This means it does a good job at learning patterns without overfitting, and it still makes accurate predictions on new, unseen data. Looking at the confusion matrix, the model correctly identifies most males (38) and most females (33). It does make a few mistakes, predicting 3 males as females and 8 females as males, but overall it gets most predictions right. These results show that just knowing how degrees are distributed between science & engineering and other fields gives the model enough information to make pretty accurate guesses about gender. So, logistic regression turns out to be a solid choice for this task and could be a good starting point for any analysis involving gender and academic choices.

from sklearn import model_selection, linear_model, metrics
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

logistic_df = filtered_df.copy()
logistic_df['Sex'] = logistic_df['Sex'].map({'Male': 0, 'Female': 1})

X = logistic_df[['Science and Engineering', 'Non-S&E']]
y = logistic_df[['Sex']]  

X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = model_selection.train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.2, random_state=111)

reg = linear_model.LogisticRegression(max_iter=1000).fit(X_train, y_train.values.ravel())

print("Train Score:", reg.score(X_train, y_train))
print("Test Score:", reg.score(X_test, y_test))

y_pred = reg.predict(X_test)
print("Accuracy Score:", metrics.accuracy_score(y_test, y_pred))

labels = np.unique(y_test)
cm = metrics.confusion_matrix(y_test, y_pred, labels=labels)
print(pd.DataFrame(cm, index=labels, columns=labels))

Ethical Considerations

I didnt find any major ethical issues. The information is presented in a way that protects peoples privacy, as it is aggregated by gender, age, and state, with no names or personal identifiers, so no individual data is exposed.

  • In terms of respect for persons, the dataset treats individuals anonymously and because there are no names or specific identities are involved, theres no risk of exposing anyones private data.

  • Under beneficence, the data is useful for spotting patterns like differences in degree attainment by gender or age. Which can help guide further analysis or inform discussions around equity in education. As long as its used carefully and interpreted responsibly, it supports the goal of using data for good.

  • For justice, the dataset seems to fairly represent a range of populations by including different genders, age groups, and states. While it doesn't directly address causes of disparity, it allows for examining trends without bias in the data itself.

  • Finally, from the perspective of respect for law and public interest, this dataset is publicly available and appears to comply with ethical and legal standards. Since it's based on national education statistics, using it to understand educational outcomes aligns with serving the public good.

Conclusion

The analysis revealed clear gender gaps in Science and Engineering degrees across U.S. states, with men earning more degrees than women in most cases. This gap was not only noticeable but also measurable and predictable using factors like major type and state, as demonstrated by both regression and classification models. The ridge regression model, with an R of about 0.97, and the logistic regression model, with approximately 87 percent accuracy, both showed strong predictive performance. This indicates that the gender disparity in STEM fields is likely driven by underlying systemic factors rather than random variation. Looking ahead, it is important not just to identify these gaps but also to promote solutions. Programs that support gender inclusion in STEM, mentorship opportunities, and early exposure to science education can help create a more balanced future.
